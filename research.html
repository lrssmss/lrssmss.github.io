Research statement

Past, current and future interests.

My research sits at the meeting point of private international law, European law, and the new fields where technology forces classical legal categories to be rethought. Across two decades I have tried to keep one question in view: how does a legal order preserve coherence and justice when the situations it governs cross borders, jurisdictions, and, increasingly, the physical world itself?

Past research

My early and continuing work is methodological. I have been concerned with the sources of private international law and with how they interact when they multiply, a theme I developed in La multilatéralisation des sources du droit international privé (SFDI/Pedone, 2023). The governing conviction is that method is not a technicality but the place where the values of a legal system are actually decided: the choice of connecting factor, the treatment of foreign law, and the role assigned to the judge each carry a substantive vision of justice. That last point, the place of the judge in European construction, I set out in the Cahiers de la Justice (2022).

Alongside this I have maintained a sustained engagement with European Union private international law as a living body of rules, most visibly through the annual chronicle on EU private international law in the Journal du droit international and through my Cours de droit international et européen, now in its seventh edition and revised each year since 2019. Teaching and doctrine here feed each other: keeping the course current forces a yearly reckoning with the state of the field.

I have also worked at the edges of the discipline, on the economic attractiveness of legal systems, particularly in Southeast Asia (RDAI, 2024), and on European environmental policy, where I contributed a volume examining how EU competence and instruments shape environmental governance.

Current research

My current work is organised around three axes.

First, the methodology of EU private international law, extending the multilateralisation project toward the coordination of overlapping European and international instruments and the coherence of the resulting system.

Second, cyberspace and international law, which I am co-directing as a 2026 Centre for Studies and Research at the Hague Academy of International Law with Professor Mohamed S. Helal (Ohio State). The question is how a body of law built for territory and physical borders governs a domain that has neither, and what the private international law dimension of cyberspace looks like once we move past the public international law framing that has dominated the debate.

Third, space law, where I am pursuing the same underlying interest: how private law reasoning, on liability, property, and applicable law, applies to activities that occur beyond any state's territory.

Future research

Looking ahead, I want to bring these strands together into a broader inquiry into private international law for non-territorial spaces: cyberspace, outer space, and the environment as a shared domain, treated not as three separate curiosities but as a single challenge to a discipline whose central tool has always been physical location. The connecting factor, the workhorse of the field, loses its footing when there is no territory to connect to, and I believe this is where the next generation of doctrinal work lies.

I also intend to deepen the comparative and economic dimension of this work, building on the Southeast Asia research, to understand how different legal systems compete and cooperate in governing cross-border and non-territorial activity, and what that means for European legal policy.

Finally, I expect the impact of artificial intelligence on legal method to become a research object in its own right, both as a subject that the law must govern and as a tool that changes how legal reasoning is produced. My own use of these tools is set out on the AI in my work page.

Interests, in short: private international law method and sources; EU private international law; cyberspace and international law; space law; European environmental policy; the economic attractiveness and comparison of legal systems.
